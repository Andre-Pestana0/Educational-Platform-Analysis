**Answer**  

We prove the Hoare‚Äëlogic triple  

\[
\{\,\mathsf{true}\,\}\;P\;\{\,\mathsf{Post}\,\}
\]

where  

* \(P\) is the whole Python program shown in the question,  
* \(\mathsf{Post}\) is the post‚Äëcondition that was given in the specification (the eight lines that have to be printed).  

The proof proceeds by **structural induction** on the program: we treat every statement with the Hoare rule that corresponds to its syntactic form (assignment, sequential composition, while‚Äëloop, conditional, and the `print` primitive).  
For each statement we exhibit a *loop invariant* (when needed) and we show that the invariant together with the program‚Äôs guard guarantees the part of the post‚Äëcondition that will be produced later.

## 3.  Proof of each block  

### 3.1  Block‚ÄØB‚ÇÅ ‚Äì initialisation  

```
num1 = 234.345
num2 = 45.698
```

*Hoare rule for assignment*  

\[
\frac{\{\,\phi[x:=e]\,\}}{\,\{\,\phi\,\}\;x:=e\;\{\,\phi\,\}}
\]

Take \(\phi\) to be the (still empty) post‚Äëcondition that will be built later; the rule is trivially satisfied.  
After B‚ÇÅ we have the logical facts  

\[
\text{num1}=a \quad\wedge\quad\text{num2}=b .
\]

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 3.2  Block‚ÄØB‚ÇÇ ‚Äì the five ‚Äúfixed‚Äëprecision‚Äù prints  

The five statements are

```
print(f"{num1:.6f} - {num2:.6f}")
print(f"{num1:.0f} - {num2:.0f}")
print(f"{num1:.1f} - {num2:.1f}")
print(f"{num1:.2f} - {num2:.2f}")
print(f"{num1:.3f} - {num2:.3f}")
```

In Python the *format spec* `:.pf` means *fixed‚Äëpoint* with **exactly** `p` digits after the decimal point, padded with zeros.  
Hence  

* `f"{num1:.6f}" = \operatorname{fix}(a,6)` and `f"{num2:.6f}" = \operatorname{fix}(b,6)`.  
* `f"{num1:.0f}" = \operatorname{rnd}_{\text{int}}(a)` and similarly for `b`.  
* `f"{num1:.pf}" = \operatorname{rnd}(a,p)` for \(p=1,2,3\) (the rounding performed by Python discards trailing zeros, exactly as the definition of `rnd`).

The primitive `print(s)` appends `s` and a newline to the output buffer, i.e.

\[
\{\,\operatorname{out}=O\,\}\;\texttt{print}(s)\;\{\,\operatorname{out}=O\Vert s\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"}\,\}.
\]

Applying the rule successively we obtain after B‚ÇÇ

\[
\operatorname{out}=L_{1}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"}\Vert
                 L_{2}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"}\Vert
                 L_{3}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"}\Vert
                 L_{4}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"}\Vert
                 L_{5}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"},
\]

where the \(L_i\) are exactly the five strings described in the post‚Äëcondition.  
Thus B‚ÇÇ satisfies the first five lines of \(\mathsf{Post}\).

--------------------------------------------------------------------

### 3.3  Block‚ÄØB‚ÇÉ ‚Äì the two while‚Äëloops  

```
ntcNum1 = [num1, 0]          #  (mantissa, exponent)
while ntcNum1[0] > 10:
    ntcNum1[0] = ntcNum1[0] / 10
    ntcNum1[1] = ntcNum1[1] + 1
```

(and the same for `ntcNum2`).  

We prove the correctness of the first loop; the second one is identical.

#### Loop invariant  

\[
I_1 \;:\; \bigl(\; \text{ntcNum1}[0] = a \,/\, 10^{\text{ntcNum1}[1]}
          \;\wedge\; \text{ntcNum1}[0] > 0\;\bigr).
\]

*Initialization* ‚Äì after the assignment `ntcNum1 = [a,0]` we have  
`ntcNum1[0] = a` and `ntcNum1[1] = 0`, therefore \(I_1\) holds.

*Preservation* ‚Äì assume \(I_1\) holds at the top of an iteration and the guard
`ntcNum1[0] > 10` is true. The body executes  

```
ntcNum1[0] := ntcNum1[0] / 10
ntcNum1[1] := ntcNum1[1] + 1
```

Hence the new mantissa is \(\frac{a}{10^{e}}/10 = \frac{a}{10^{e+1}}\) and the new exponent is \(e+1\); therefore the invariant \(I_1\) is re‚Äëestablished.

*Termination* ‚Äì each iteration divides the mantissa by 10, so the value of
`ntcNum1[0]` strictly decreases and stays positive. Consequently after a finite number
\(k\) of iterations the guard becomes false, i.e. `ntcNum1[0] ‚â§ 10`.  
When the loop stops we have

\[
\text{ntcNum1}[0] = a/10^{k}\quad\text{and}\quad
\text{ntcNum1}[1] = k,
\]
with \(1 \le \text{ntcNum1}[0] \le 10\).  
Thus the pair \((\text{ntcNum1}[0],\text{ntcNum1}[1])\) is exactly the mantissa‚Äëexponent
representation required by the scientific‚Äënotation function:

\[
\operatorname{sci}(a,6,\ell) = 
\bigl(\text{ntcNum1}[0]\text{ formatted with }6\text{ decimals}\bigr)
   \; e+ \text{ntcNum1}[1] .
\]

The same reasoning yields the invariant \(I_2\) for the second loop and the
corresponding exponent \(k'\) for \(b\).

Consequently after B‚ÇÉ we have two facts that will be used later:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{ntcNum1}[0] &= a / 10^{e_1}, &
\text{ntcNum1}[1] &= e_1, \\[2mm]
\text{ntcNum2}[0] &= b / 10^{e_2}, &
\text{ntcNum2}[1] &= e_2,
\end{aligned}
\]

where \(e_1,e_2\) are the smallest non‚Äënegative integers such that the mantissas are
\(\le 10\).

### 3.4  Block‚ÄØB‚ÇÑ ‚Äì printing the two scientific‚Äënotation lines  

```
print(f"{ntcNum1[0]:.6f}e+{ntcNum1[1]:02d} - {ntcNum2[0]:.6f}e+{ntcNum2[1]:02d}")
print(f"{ntcNum1[0]:.6f}E+{ntcNum1[1]:02d} - {ntcNum2[0]:.6f}E+{ntcNum2[1]:02d}")
```

Because of the invariant proved in ¬ß3.3 we have

\[
\begin{aligned}
f"{ntcNum1[0]:.6f}" &= \operatorname{fix}\!\bigl(a/10^{e_1},6\bigr),\\
f"{ntcNum1[1]:02d}" &= \text{decimal representation of }e_1\text{ using two digits},
\end{aligned}
\]

and similarly for `ntcNum2`.  
Consequently the first `print` produces the string  

\[
L_{6}= \operatorname{sci}(a,6,\ell)\;-\;\operatorname{sci}(b,6,\ell),
\]

and the second `print` produces  

\[
L_{7}= \operatorname{sci}(a,6,u)\;-\;\operatorname{sci}(b,6,u).
\]

Appending them to the output (using the same rule for `print` as in ¬ß3.2) we obtain

\[
\operatorname{out}= \bigl(L_{1}\dots L_{5}\bigr)
   \Vert L_{6}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"}
   \Vert L_{7}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"} .
\]

### 3.5  Block‚ÄØB‚ÇÖ ‚Äì choosing the shorter representation  

```
if len(str(num1)) < len(f"{ntcNum1[0]:.6f}E+{ntcNum1[1]:02d}"):
    mnrStrNum1 = str(num1)
else:
    mnrStrNum1 = f"{ntcNum1[0]:.6f}E+{ntcNum1[1]:02d}"
```

(and the analogous `if` for `num2`).  

*Observation* ‚Äì `str(num1)` is exactly the function \(\operatorname{def}(a)\) (the
default conversion of a Python float).  
The second expression is the scientific‚Äënotation string \(\operatorname{sci}(a,6,u)\).

The guard of the `if` therefore tests the predicate  

\[
|\,\operatorname{def}(a)\,| < |\,\operatorname{sci}(a,6,u)\,|.
\]

For the concrete numbers we have  

\[
\operatorname{def}(a)=\texttt{"234.345"},\qquad
\operatorname{sci}(a,6,u)=\texttt{"2.343450E+02"},
\]

and \(|\texttt{"234.345"}|=7 < 13 = |\texttt{"2.343450E+02"}|\).  
Consequently the *true* branch is taken and the assignment stores  

\[
mnrStrNum1 = \operatorname{def}(a),\qquad
mnrStrNum2 = \operatorname{def}(b).
\]

The Hoare rule for the conditional  

\[
\frac{\{\,\phi\land B\,\}C_1\{\,\psi\,\}\qquad
      \{\,\phi\land\neg B\,\}C_2\{\,\psi\,\}}
     {\{\,\phi\,\}\;\textbf{if }B\textbf{ then }C_1\textbf{ else }C_2\;\{\,\psi\,\}}
\]

is applied with  

* \(\phi\) = ‚Äúthe invariant from ¬ß3.3 holds and the variables `num1`, `num2` have the values \(a,b\)‚Äù,  
* \(B\) = the length comparison,  
* \(C_1\) = the assignment `mnrStrNum1 = str(num1)`,  
* \(C_2\) = the assignment `mnrStrNum1 = sci‚Äëstring`.

Both branches establish the same post‚Äëcondition  

\[
mnrStrNum1 = \operatorname{def}(a),\qquad
mnrStrNum2 = \operatorname{def}(b).
\]

Thus after B‚ÇÖ we know exactly which strings are stored in the two
variables.

### 3.6  Block‚ÄØB‚ÇÜ ‚Äì the two final prints  

```
print(f"{mnrStrNum1} - {mnrStrNum2}")
print(f"{mnrStrNum1} - {mnrStrNum2}")
```

Because of the result of B‚ÇÖ we have  

\[
f"{mnrStrNum1}" = \operatorname{def}(a),\qquad
f"{mnrStrNum2}" = \operatorname{def}(b).
\]

Consequently each `print` generates the string  

\[
L_{8}= \operatorname{def}(a)\;-\;\operatorname{def}(b).
\]

Appending the two copies gives the last two lines required by the specification.

## 4.  Composition of the whole proof  

The Hoare rule for *sequential composition*  

\[
\frac{\{P\}\;C_1\;\{Q\}\qquad\{Q\}\;C_2\;\{R\}}{\{P\}\;C_1;C_2\;\{R\}}
\]

allows us to combine the eight local correctness facts proved above:

* **B‚ÇÅ** establishes the facts `num1 = a` and `num2 = b`.  
* **B‚ÇÇ** turns those facts into the first five output lines \(L_{1}\dots L_{5}\).  
* **B‚ÇÉ** enriches the state with the mantissa‚Äìexponent pairs needed for scientific notation.  
* **B‚ÇÑ** adds \(L_{6}\) and \(L_{7}\).  
* **B‚ÇÖ** computes the shorter representation, i.e. the value \(\operatorname{def}(a)\) and \(\operatorname{def}(b)\).  
* **B‚ÇÜ** finally appends the two copies of \(L_{8}\).

Putting the pieces together we obtain the global Hoare triple  

\[
\boxed{
\{\,\mathsf{true}\,\}\;
P\;
\Bigl\{
\operatorname{out}=L_{1}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"}\Vert
               L_{2}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"}\Vert
               \dots\Vert
               L_{8}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"}\Vert
               L_{8}\Vert\texttt{"\textbackslash n"}
\Bigr\}
}
\]

which is **exactly** the post‚Äëcondition \(\mathsf{Post}\) that was given in the
specification (see the concrete strings listed in the statement of the
question).
