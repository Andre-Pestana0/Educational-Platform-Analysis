Below is a **Hoare‑logic style specification** of the whole task (reading the two numbers, computing the remainder and printing it).  
Only the *pre‑condition* (what must be true before the program starts) and the *post‑condition* (what must be true after it terminates) are given; the body of the program is left abstract (it can be any algorithm that satisfies the contract).

---

### Variables  

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| `A`    | first integer read from the input |
| `B`    | second integer read from the input |
| `R`    | integer that will be printed (the remainder) |
| `q`    | auxiliary integer (quotient) that may appear in the post‑condition |

All variables range over the set of integers **ℤ**.

---

### Pre‑condition  \(P\)

\[
\boxed{P \;:=\; 
\begin{aligned}
& A \in \mathbb Z \;\land\; B \in \mathbb Z \\
& \land\; 1 \le A \le 100\,000 \\
& \land\; 1 \le B \le 100\,000
\end{aligned}}
\]

Interpretation: the program receives two **valid** input integers, each between 1 and 100 000 inclusive.

---

### Post‑condition  \(Q\)

\[
\boxed{Q \;:=\; 
\begin{aligned}
& R \in \mathbb Z \\
& \land\; \exists q \in \mathbb Z \;.\; A = B\cdot q + R \\
& \land\; 0 \le R < B
\end{aligned}}
\]

Equivalently, \(R = A \bmod B\).  
The three conjuncts guarantee:

* `R` is an integer,
* `A` can be expressed as `B·q + R` for some integer `q` (division algorithm),
* the remainder lies in the canonical range \([0,\,B-1]\), which makes it **unique**.

---

### Hoare Triple

\[
\boxed{\{\,P\,\}\; \texttt{program}\; \{\,Q\,\}}
\]

where `program` is any sequence of statements that:

1. reads `A`,
2. reads `B`,
3. computes a value `R` satisfying the conditions of \(Q\) (e.g. `R := A - B * \lfloor A / B \rfloor`),
4. prints `R`.

---

### Remarks  

* The pre‑condition restricts the domain exactly as required by the problem statement (`1 ≤ A,B ≤ 100 000`).  
* The post‑condition captures the **whole** specification of the output: it is the *unique* remainder of the Euclidean division of `A` by `B`.  
* No other side‑effects (e.g., extra output, modification of `A` or `B`) are permitted by the specification; they are implicitly forbidden because they are not mentioned in \(Q\).

This formalisation can be used as the contract for verification, testing, or for proving the correctness of a concrete implementation (e.g., in C, Python, Java, etc.).
